Stabilizing Language Model Training Without Flattening Every Failure Into “Gradient Explosion”

This handbook argues that language-model instability is not one problem but a family of control surfaces: residual and norm geometry, attention-logit collapse, output-head divergence, numeric-path hygiene, and repetition that may be decode-side rather than train-side. Central Thesis

The first diagnosis must be “which tensor path failed first?”

Lowering the learning rate, clipping harder, or adding a repetition penalty are common reactions because they are easy to apply. They are also often wrong first moves. The rerun bundle reinforced the same conclusion as the earlier research: each intervention family belongs to a different failure surface, so the book is organized by diagnosis before prescription.

Evidence Posture

What is already strong and what remains partial

  • ready baseline recipe, diagnosis, attention-logit controls, numeric hygiene, and repetition-vs-decoding separation.
  • partial DeepNorm coefficient appendix coverage and OEC as a production-ready replacement for z-loss.
  • frontier SPAM as a default optimizer intervention and omni-model transfer claims for modality-balanced training.

Reading Path

If you are debugging a run

Read Chapters 1 and 2 first. Then go to the chapter matching the first abnormal metric: residual, attention, LM head, numeric path, or decoding. The intended order is diagnostic, not encyclopedic. The book is arranged to stop you from spending Chapter 10 time on a Chapter 3 problem.

Reading Path

If you are designing a baseline recipe

Read Chapter 3 first, then Chapters 4, 5, 7, and 8. Leave Chapter 10 for last because it is a cautionary maturity chapter, not a baseline cookbook. If your stack still lacks basic packing, numeric-path hygiene, or z-loss, the frontier chapter is almost certainly too early.

How To Read

Use the book like a lab manual, not like a trend map

The organizing question in every chapter is the same: which tensor path failed first, what is the first metric to inspect, and what is the cheapest coherent intervention that matches that failure? This is why the book puts taxonomy and monitoring before mechanisms, and stable defaults before frontier methods.
