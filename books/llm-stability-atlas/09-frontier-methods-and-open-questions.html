Chapter 9

Frontier Methods and Open Questions

This chapter is not a second baseline recipe. Its job is narrower: to place the newer methods, especially SPAM and output embedding centering, into a realistic judgment frame. The main conclusion is that these ideas are promising, but the older public controls still have the stronger large-scale precedent. SPAM OEC PaLM

Why This Chapter Exists

Every stability literature accumulates newer proposals that sound like replacements for the previous recipe. That is not always how they should be read. This chapter exists to separate “interesting and potentially important” from “ready to displace the current public default.”

Intuition

Frontier methods usually target the hardest remaining part of the problem: the rare failure that survives a sane base architecture, sane numeric path, and sane decoding. That is why they are attractive. It is also why their evidence base tends to be thinner: the problem is rarer, and the public large-scale comparisons are harder to publish cleanly.

Methods

SPAM

SPAM is an optimizer-level response to rare spikes, using spike-aware clipping and momentum reset. It is most interesting when the architecture is already fixed and the remaining failure is rare but catastrophic. SPAM

OEC

Output embedding centering argues that output-head geometry is the deeper issue behind some logit-instability regimes. It is a more ambitious claim than z-loss, but the public evidence in this bundle is still at a more limited maturity level. OEC PaLM

Concrete Comparison

Method What it targets Why it is interesting Current judgment
SPAM Rare catastrophic spikes Optimizer-level intervention when architecture is fixed Promising but not first-line before baseline stack is correct
OEC Output-head geometry Potentially deeper fix than z-loss [UNCERTAIN] Frontier-scale validation remains thinner than z-loss precedent

Strengths

  • These methods ask sharper questions than the older generic “better training” literature.
  • They target exactly the residual failures that remain after a strong base recipe is in place.
  • They can guide future experimentation when a stable baseline still leaves rare spikes or output-head fragility unresolved.

Limitations And Failure Cases

  • The public evidence base is younger and usually smaller than for Pre-LN, DeepNorm, Q/K normalization, or z-loss.
  • It is easy to overread these methods as total replacements rather than specialist tools.
  • Some key comparisons remain genuinely open, including Q/K normalization versus soft-capping at long context and OEC versus z-loss at larger scale.

Practical Notes

The conservative deployment order remains: build the baseline stack, verify diagnosis metrics, then test frontier methods in controlled experiments. If a team is still on Post-LN, still missing z-loss, or still running fragile FP16 without FP32-sensitive operations, this chapter is not where they should start. PaLM Xiong 2020

Open Questions

  • Can OEC replace z-loss cleanly at 7B–70B+ scale?
  • Which attention-logit stabilizer gives the best stability / throughput trade-off on long-context dense decoders?
  • How much of the rare late-spike problem is optimizer-state hysteresis versus output-head geometry?
  • For omni models, does the dominant instability shift to modality-balancing losses? [MISSING FROM SOURCE]
