Chapter 4

Residual and Depth Geometry

This chapter covers the oldest and still most important stability distinction in Transformer training: Post-LN versus Pre-LN, and the broader question of how residual updates scale with depth. The main conclusion is pragmatic. Pre-LN or a closely related RMSNorm design should be the default. DeepNorm or large-shortcut designs become necessary only when depth or long-run amplification still remains a problem. Xiong 2020 DeepNet Spike No More

Why This Chapter Exists

Many training failures are easiest to prevent before they appear, by choosing residual geometry that does not create bad gradients at initialization and does not quietly amplify updates across many layers. The chapter exists because teams sometimes treat this as an architectural taste issue when the public literature treats it as a first-order stability choice.

Intuition

The residual stream is the backbone that carries [B, T, D] hidden states through the network. If the sublayer updates are too aggressive relative to the shortcut, then gradients and activations can become hard to manage. Pre-LN helps early because normalization happens before the sublayer. DeepNorm helps later or deeper by explicitly making the shortcut large relative to the new update.

Formal Mechanics

A standard Pre-LN block can be written as:

u_l = x_l + Attn(Norm(x_l))

x_(l+1) = u_l + MLP(Norm(u_l))

By contrast, the DeepNorm form reported in GLM-130B is:

DeepNorm(x) = LayerNorm(αx + Network(x))

α = √(2N)

GLM-130B further reports scaling Xavier initialization for ffn, v_proj, and out_proj by (2N)^(-1/2). Example

The simplest decision rule is this: if a decoder stack is modest in depth and still using Post-LN, switching to Pre-LN or an RMSNorm variant is the first architectural change. If the stack is already Pre-LN but still exhibits long-horizon amplification or depth-heavy late spikes, then DeepNorm or another large-shortcut design becomes the next structural move.

GLM-130B adds one more concrete lesson: the embedding path can be its own failure channel. Their report states that shrinking the embedding gradient by a factor of 0.1 solved most numerical-instability problems in that stack. GLM-130B

Strengths

  • Pre-LN has strong theoretical and practical support as a default baseline.
  • DeepNorm gives a principled path to much deeper stable stacks.
  • The family directly addresses gradient behavior rather than only constraining bad updates after they occur.

Limitations And Failure Cases

  • Residual geometry alone does not solve attention entropy collapse or output-logit divergence.
  • DeepNorm introduces extra tuning burden and may be needless complexity for shallow or mid-depth stacks.
  • The exact coefficient mapping across all architecture variants remains partly [UNCERTAIN] without a fuller appendix pass.

Practical Notes

Treat Pre-LN as the default and DeepNorm as the escalation. Do not reverse that order unless you are inheriting a fixed architecture and already know that depth is the binding constraint. Also watch the embedding layer separately. A single outlier layer family can dominate stability behavior even when the rest of the residual stream looks acceptable.
