Chapter 8

Repetition and Decoding

Repetition is a distinct problem because it can emerge even when the training dynamics are otherwise stable. This chapter argues that the first task is diagnostic separation: determine whether the loop is decode-induced or whether the model has actually learned an overly repetition-heavy distribution. Only after that split is made do decoding changes or unlikelihood training make sense. Holtzman Welleck

Why This Chapter Exists

Teams often overreact to repeating tokens by changing training immediately. That can be a mistake. Holtzman et al. show that decode-time maximization can itself produce repetitive loops even for a fixed model. Welleck et al. show that train-time unlikelihood can help when repetition really is in the learned distribution. The chapter exists to stop those two cases from being conflated. Holtzman Welleck

Intuition

Decoding and training are two different control layers. If greedy or beam-like decoding repeatedly picks the same high-probability continuation, the model may not be numerically unstable at all. If the model repeats across sane stochastic decoders as well, then the learned distribution itself may be the problem. Nucleus sampling is therefore not only a style choice. It is a diagnostic tool.

Formal Mechanics

The canonical unlikelihood loss for a set of negative candidates C_t is:

L_UL = - Σ_(c ∈ C_t) log(1 - p(c | x_<t))

This is qualitatively different from decode-side controls such as top-p sampling. Unlikelihood changes the learned distribution. Top-p sampling changes how the next token is selected from the learned distribution.

Example

The fastest practical diagnostic sequence is:

  1. Generate with greedy or beam-like settings and note the loop.
  2. Generate the same prompt with nucleus sampling.
  3. If the loop disappears, treat the problem first as decode-side degeneration.
  4. If repetition persists under sane stochastic decoding, inspect [eod] packing and consider unlikelihood training.

A decode artifact is not evidence that the optimizer or numeric path was broken. That is why this chapter comes after the core stability chapters rather than before them.

Strengths

  • The chapter gives a clean operational decision boundary between decode-side and train-side remedies.
  • Nucleus sampling is lightweight and informative as a diagnosis step.
  • Unlikelihood training provides a principled train-time mechanism rather than only heuristic repetition penalties.

Limitations And Failure Cases

  • Not every repetitive generation issue is solved by top-p sampling or unlikelihood alone.
  • Boundary-token mistakes and packing artifacts can still masquerade as repetition pathologies.
  • Decode-time remedies can hide a genuinely bad model if they are treated as the only evaluation condition.

Practical Notes

Treat nucleus sampling as a first diagnostic before changing training. Verify explicit [eod] handling. If repetition remains under sane sampling, then unlikelihood training becomes a serious option. If repetition disappears once decoding is changed, do not immediately complicate the training objective. Holtzman Welleck PaLM
